Notas previas para el presentador

Audiencia: Management interno SQLI (partners, delivery leads, ops, sponsors). Objetivo: aprobación para invertir en construir la plataforma — no para venderle a un cliente externo.

Tono

Confiado pero honesto. Esto no es marketing — es un plan de inversión. La audiencia espera ver el plan, no el sueño.

Tres líneas de defensa contra preguntas críticas

  • “¿Está funcionando hoy?” — “Plano 1 con Claude Code está funcionando hoy y validado con Muta. Plano 2 y Plano 3 los probamos con un experimento concreto cada uno. Hoy estoy aquí pidiendo construir Phase 2.”
  • “¿Quién compra esto?” — “Esto no se le vende al cliente. Es cómo SQLI delivera. El cliente compra un proyecto que sale más rápido y más barato. La plataforma es nuestra ventaja competitiva interna.”
  • “¿Cuánto cuesta y cuánto tarda?” — Cifras concretas en la slide de roadmap. Phase 1 es donde estamos. Phase 2 son meses, no años.

Cosas a NO decir

  • “Va a revolucionar la industria” — vacío
  • “Esto es lo que está haciendo todo el mundo” — falso y débil
  • “El agente reemplaza al equipo” — peligroso y no es lo que estamos vendiendo

Cosa a decir cuando dudás

“No tengo la respuesta cerrada todavía. Lo voy a documentar antes del próximo paso.” — eso construye confianza, no la rompe.

Pre-presentation notes

Audience: SQLI internal management (partners, delivery leads, ops, sponsors). Goal: approval to invest in building the platform — not selling to an external client.

Tone

Three lines of defense against critical questions

  • “Is this working today?” — “Plane 1 with Claude Code is working today and validated with Muta. Plane 2 and Plane 3 we'll prove with one concrete experiment each. Today I'm here asking to build Phase 2.”
  • “Who buys this?” — “This isn't sold to clients. It's how SQLI delivers. The client buys a project that ships faster and cheaper. The platform is our internal competitive advantage.”
  • “How much does it cost and how long does it take?” — Concrete numbers on the roadmap slide. Phase 1 is where we are. Phase 2 is months, not years.

Things NOT to say

  • “It will revolutionize the industry” — empty
  • “This is what everyone is doing” — false and weak
  • “The agent replaces the team” — dangerous, and it's not what we're selling

What to say when you don't know

“I don't have a closed answer yet. I'll document it before the next step.” — that builds trust; it doesn't break it.

Cuando alguien escucha “agente” piensa en cosas distintas. Acá hay cinco capacidades concretas, en orden ascendente de autonomía y riesgo.

Knows — memoria institucional. Cualquiera pregunta “¿por qué elegimos este stack?” y el agente lee el ADR y responde con la decisión, alternativas, y fecha. Read-only.

Reports — estado del proyecto sintetizado a través de sistemas. “¿Cómo va el sprint?” cruza Jira, GitHub y observability en una respuesta con riesgos flagged. Read-only.

Creates — artefactos. Brand systems desde packaging físico, mid-fi designs, copy, prompts de imagen, código migrado. Multi-modal output. El agente no solo orquesta — produce.

Operates — trabajo rutinario auditado. Status reports a Confluence. Triage de tickets. Diagnosis de builds rotos en PR comments. Low-blast.

Acts — acciones privilegiadas. Deploys, scaling, migraciones. Acá el agente prepara, presenta el riesgo, y espera aprobación humana out-of-band. La enforcement no está en las instrucciones del agente — está en la arquitectura del MCP. Volveré a este punto en safety.

When someone hears “agent” they think of different things. Here are five concrete capabilities, in ascending order of autonomy and risk.

Knows — institutional memory. Anyone asks “why did we choose this stack?” and the agent reads the ADR and responds with the decision, alternatives, and date. Read-only.

Reports — project state synthesized across systems. “How's the sprint going?” cross-references Jira, GitHub and observability into a single answer with risks flagged. Read-only.

Creates — artifacts. Brand systems from physical packaging, mid-fidelity designs, copy, image prompts, migrated code. Multi-modal output. The agent doesn't just orchestrate — it produces.

Operates — auditable routine work. Status reports to Confluence. Ticket triage. Diagnosis of broken builds in PR comments. Low-blast.

Acts — privileged actions. Deploys, scaling, migrations. Here the agent prepares, presents the risk, and waits for out-of-band human approval. Enforcement isn't in the agent's instructions — it's in the MCP architecture. I'll come back to this in the safety section.

Three planes of operation

Three planes of operation

1:45

Esta es la slide más importante operacionalmente. El sustrato corre en tres planos, cada uno con su operador, su tecnología, y su razón de existir.

Plano 1 — Engineering. Operador: el SQLI engineer con Claude Code en su máquina. Patrón: interactivo, iterativo. Para qué: brand, código, ADRs, decisiones, provisioning de nuevos engagements vía /new-client, kickoff, todo lo nuevo o exploratorio. Es donde el juicio humano vale.

Para decidir el plano, tres preguntas. ¿Requiere juicio del engineer en el loop? Plano 1. ¿Es una acción única triggered por evento? Plano 2. ¿Es un batch de items similares con multi-step processing? Plano 3. Cada workload tiene una respuesta correcta.

[click]

This is the most important slide operationally. The substrate runs on three planes, each with its operator, its technology, and its reason for existing.

Plane 1 — Engineering. Operator: the SQLI engineer with Claude Code on their machine. Pattern: interactive, iterative. For: brand, code, ADRs, decisions, provisioning new engagements via /new-client, kickoff, anything new or exploratory. It's where human judgment matters.

Plane 2 — Automation. Operator: a trigger — cron or webhook. No human in the loop. Pattern: atomic, predictable, autonomous. For: status reports, PR reviews, ticket triage, alerts. Tasks that move to the Managed Agents runtime to run on their own when they should.

Plane 3 — Agent Team. Operator: an engineer kicks off, then it runs on its own. Pattern: bounded batch, multi-step per item, multi-agent loop. For: page generation, test generation, content at volume. Also lives on Managed Agents but with multi-agent coordination and shared memory.

To pick the plane, three questions. Does it require the engineer's judgment in the loop? Plane 1. Is it a bounded action triggered by an event? Plane 2. Is it a batch of similar items needing multi-step processing? Plane 3. Each workload has one right answer.

Slide 11 · s6g

Plane 3 In Action

Plane 3 In Action

Agent teams: from a batch to a delivery

Agent teams: from a batch to a delivery

2:00

Voy a aterrizar Plano 3 con un ejemplo concreto. La idea: el engineer prepara los inputs, el equipo de agentes corre por horas, los outputs caen en sistemas durables, los humanos revisan en checkpoints.

Imaginen una tarea típica de Plano 3: generar y refrescar el test suite del engagement a partir de 40 user stories — E2E, unit, con detección de flakes y sugerencias de fix. Imposible de hacer bien a mano. Demasiado coordinado para una automation única.

Paso uno: el engineer prepara el batch. Empuja el test inventory, las convenciones del framework (Playwright, Jest), targets de coverage, y el histórico de flakes al Memory Store del engagement. Define el goal.

Paso dos: el equipo se despliega. Un lead agent lee la cola y despacha specialists por etapa — TestWriter genera E2E desde user stories, UnitWriter genera unit tests desde el código, TestExecutor corre la suite y captura fails, FailClassifier separa flakes de fails reales, FixSuggestor propone fixes. Cada specialist trabaja en paralelo o secuencia según corresponda.

Paso tres: los outputs caen en sistemas durables. Commits a GitHub con tests nuevos y actualizados. Update al Memory Store registrando inventory y flake history. Test report en Confluence visible a stakeholders. Loop continúa a la siguiente user story.

Paso cuatro: cada N items, checkpoint. La sesión pausa, postea un digest. El engineer aprueba, ajusta, o detiene. El loop sigue desde el siguiente item si está green-lit. Horas de trabajo, minutos de atención humana.

Mismo patrón aplica para content production a volumen, code migration a volumen, page generation. Donde Claude Code llega a su techo — loops largos con estado compartido — los agent teams toman.

[click]

I'll ground Plane 3 with a concrete example. The idea: the engineer prepares inputs, the agent team runs for hours, outputs fall into durable systems, humans review at checkpoints.

Imagine a typical Plane 3 task: generate and refresh the engagement's test suite from 40 user stories — E2E, unit, with flake detection and fix suggestions. Impossible to do well by hand. Too coordinated for a single automation.

Step one: the engineer prepares the batch. Pushes test inventory, framework conventions (Playwright, Jest), coverage targets, and known-flake history to the engagement's Memory Store. Defines the goal.

Step two: the team deploys. A lead agent reads the queue and dispatches specialists per stage — TestWriter generates E2E from user stories, UnitWriter generates unit tests from code, TestExecutor runs the suite and captures fails, FailClassifier separates flakes from real fails, FixSuggestor proposes fixes. Each specialist works in parallel or in sequence as appropriate.

Step three: outputs fall into durable systems. GitHub commits with new and updated tests. A Memory Store update with inventory and flake history. A test report on Confluence visible to stakeholders. The loop continues to the next user story.

Step four: every N items, checkpoint. The session pauses, posts a digest. The engineer approves, adjusts, or halts. The loop resumes from the next item if green-lit. Hours of work, minutes of human attention.

The same pattern fits content production at volume, code migration at volume, page generation. Where Claude Code hits its ceiling — long loops with shared state — agent teams take over.

“What if Anthropic changes the pricing or deprecates Managed Agents?”

El Agent SDK es código que corre en cualquier infra. La inversión está en las skills y la knowledge base, no en el runtime. Migrar es cuestión de redeployment. El lock-in en MA es bajo en lo que importa: lo que vale acumulado es nuestro. Y notar que Plano 1 (Claude Code) sigue funcionando independientemente de MA.

The Agent SDK is code that runs on any infra. The investment is in the skills and the knowledge base, not in the runtime. Migrating is a redeployment matter. Lock-in on MA is low where it counts: the accumulated value is ours. And note that Plane 1 (Claude Code) keeps working independently of MA.

“¿Por qué tres planos y no uno solo en MA?”

“Why three planes and not just one on MA?”

Porque cada plano resuelve un problema distinto. Plano 1 con Claude Code es donde el engineer tiene control total y latencia baja para trabajo creativo. Plano 2 con MA single-shot es para tareas atómicas autónomas que no necesitan humano. Plano 3 con MA agent team es para batches voluminosos que Claude Code no soporta. Forzar todo a un plano único es lo que hace que la plataforma se sienta sobreingenierada.

Because each plane solves a different problem. Plane 1 with Claude Code is where the engineer has full control and low latency for creative work. Plane 2 with MA single-shot is for atomic autonomous tasks that don't need a human. Plane 3 with MA agent team is for voluminous batches Claude Code can't handle. Forcing everything onto a single plane is what makes the platform feel over-engineered.

“Why not do this with Copilot or any other LLM?”

Copilot es asistencia de código, no orquestación de delivery. Lo que estamos construyendo es la capa de skills y conocimiento que ningún vendor tiene. El modelo de IA es commodity; el conocimiento codificado de SQLI no.

Copilot is code assistance, not delivery orchestration. What we're building is the skills and knowledge layer that no vendor has. The AI model is commodity; SQLI's codified knowledge is not.

Por cliente activo, entre cien y quinientos dólares por mes en costos de Managed Agents, dependiendo de actividad de Plano 2 y 3. Plano 1 es el costo de Claude Code por engineer. Tickets de €100K-€500K. La economía cierra con margen.

Per active client, between one hundred and five hundred dollars per month in Managed Agents costs, depending on Plane 2 and 3 activity. Plane 1 is the Claude Code cost per engineer. €100K-€500K tickets. The economics close with margin.

Plano 1 es interactivo: el engineer está en el loop, lo cual ya es safety. La diferencia con PocketOS es que la enforcement no está en las instrucciones del agente, está en la arquitectura del MCP.

Plane 1 is interactive: the engineer is in the loop, which is already safety. The difference with PocketOS is that enforcement isn't in the agent's instructions — it's in the MCP architecture.
